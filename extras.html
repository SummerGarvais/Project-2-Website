Proposition: Increased energy consumption leads to higher gas prices.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • -1.5: I filtered to 2004 net energy importers. This is still selective, but the constraint follows the proposition more directly: when countries rely on imported energy, higher consumption is more likely to expose them to higher fuel prices.
  • 1: I used a country-level scatterplot with a fitted trend line so the positive association is readable without hiding individual observations. The marks still show exceptions, while the line makes the intended conclusion immediate.
  • -0.5: I used a log scale for energy use per person. This keeps low- and high-consumption countries in the same frame and makes the upward relationship easier to see across a wide range of energy use.
  • 0.5: The color split compares the lower- and higher-energy halves and pairs it with a median-price callout. This helps readers remember the main numerical contrast, though it steers attention toward a binary framing.

FOR the Proposition: Checkpoint View

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • 0.5: This checkpoint view uses electric power consumption per capita as a proxy for energy consumption. That makes the relationship easier to interpret for readers, though it narrows the meaning of "energy" to electricity rather than all fuels.
  • 1: The chart keeps a country-level scatterplot and trend line, which makes the positive relationship visible while preserving outliers. The higher-power half median callout gives the viewer a quick numerical takeaway.
  • -0.5: The log-scaled x-axis compresses the huge difference between low- and high-power countries. This is reasonable for the range of values, but it also makes the upward pattern feel steadier than it would on a linear axis.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • -1.5: I filtered to net energy exporters in 2014, where the relationship cuts against the proposition. This is persuasive because high-consumption fuel-producing countries often have cheaper domestic fuel, but it is selective because it leaves out many high-income energy importers with high gasoline prices.
  • 1: I used a scatterplot with country-level marks and a fitted trend line so the downward direction is visible without hiding individual observations. This makes the counterargument feel data-driven while still showing exceptions like Norway.
  • -0.5: The x-axis uses a log scale for energy use per capita. This makes small and very large energy users fit cleanly in one chart and visually strengthens the downward pattern by spacing high-energy exporters more evenly.
  • 0.5: The color split highlights the higher-energy-use half in orange and the lower half in gray. This supports quick comparison of the two medians, though it nudges the reader toward the intended high-versus-low framing.

AGAINST the Proposition: Checkpoint View

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • 0.5: This checkpoint view groups country-level points by broad World Bank region and samples each region across its power-consumption range. That mirrors the checkpoint approach more closely than regional averages while keeping the number of points readable.
  • 1: Coloring by region makes it easier to see that the relationship is not uniform across geography. The legend supports the regional comparison, while country-level points preserve more detail than one aggregate point per region.
  • -0.5: The balanced regional sample is persuasive but selective: it avoids letting regions with many countries dominate the chart. That helps the against side by making cross-region exceptions visible, but it also means this view is not the full country-level distribution.
