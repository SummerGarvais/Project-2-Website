Proposition: Increased energy consumption leads to higher gas prices.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Splitting countries into a less energy consuming half and more energy consuming half allows us to highlight the most energy consuming countries. Making them a brighter color than the less consuming countries draws focus to them and affirms the proposition.
  • The labeled countries at the top again draw focus to those countries which have significantly higher gas prices due to energy consumption.
  • Deliberately not highlighting countries which fall below the line mitigates arguments to the contrary.
  • Comparing the medians between the two halves shows a very cut and dry difference as well.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Using gallons on the y-axis as opposed to liters helps form a seemingly larger negative trend when compared to the liter graph.
  • We found selecting the top 5 energy-intensive countries in each region would increase this downward trend, but is still a reasonable representation of the region.
  • Keeping the standard error on the line gives some ambiguity as to how harsh the downward trend is, perhaps leading people to believe that the downward trend is greater than it really is.

Final Reflection

Throughout the design process, we began by first identifying plausible relationships to visualize, such as the connections that fuel costs might have with natural resource usage or energy consumption. While many different and intriguing relationships existed within our chosen dataset, what proved to be more interesting was recognizing how easily these relationships could be manipulated depending on how we chose to define a given variable. For example, in the first proposition, using total natural resource rents appeared to show a negative correlation with gas prices, but this also ignored the fact that oil production within those countries was the primary driver behind that relationship. Similarly, in the second proposition, choosing between two different metrics to define a nation's energy consumption drew entirely opposite conclusions. It was interesting and slightly alarming to see how both visualizations could remain technically "correct" while still presenting fundamentally different interpretations of the same data.

This exercise in data visualization "p-hacking" has reshaped how we define ethical analysis and visualization. Ethical visualization is not just about using authentic data, making sure it was sourced ethically, or intentionally incorrect calculations, but about presenting information in a way that does not mislead the viewer through selective framing. While the entire purpose of data visualization is to persuade viewers of a given idea, the boundary between acceptable and misleading choices lies in whether the visualization intentionally hides important assumptions or relevant variables. In our case, excluding the role of oil in natural resource rents or selectively choosing energy metrics crosses into misleading territory because it hides important context that may impact the viewer's opinion. Although it is unrealistic to draw a clear border on what makes a visualization ethical, we now view ethical visualization as a commitment to transparency, where the goal is not just to support a claim, but to accurately represent the complexity behind it rather than portraying it in a way that distorts the truth.
